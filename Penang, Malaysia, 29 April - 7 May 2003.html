Penang, Malaysia, 29 April - 7 May 2003

A little Singapore with a hill
By David

We caught the high speed ferry from Medan in Indonesia to Penang in Malaysia; a pleasant if somewhat boring journey - the ferry company is Malaysian you see. Penang is an island similar to Singapore but up on the north west coast of Malaysia. It is connected to the mainland by a 3 kilometre bridge; in true Malaysian style it must be the longest (of some kind) in the world.

The city of Georgetown (the major settlement on Penang) was actually the first major British colony in the Indies. The British governor who founded the place encouraged people to live there by firing silver dollars into the forest from his ships canons! At some stage the Chinese arrived, and like in Singapore it is them who make up the majority of the population; and ensure that Georgetown is a clean, bustling capitalist city. Add to that the British legacy of colonial buildings and English street names, and you can see why we found Georgetown to be so similar to Singapore.

Penang has a very pleasant café life style, where you wonder down the road in the morning, pop into on of the many cafés selling breakfast to eat and catch up on the mornings news on CNN, before heading off to do your daily thing. Typically that thing would be shopping; all the streets are lined with shops, and there are three staggeringly large inter-connected shopping malls to loose yourselves in. The food was pleasantly western, and we even managed to find an African restaurant, run by a guy from Benin, where we ended up eating a large proportion of our meals. Partly this was because it was a relief to hear African sounds again, and to see black people.

In true eastern style, Penang has an amazing selection of cutting edge, cheap electronics (not to mention store upon store selling pirated software). We were able to pick up a Canon camera for my mum at almost a 1/3rd of the price it would have cost in SA. (And it survived being posted home, even though someone poked a hole right through the fragile label!). Clothes turned out to be a harder buy; in particular for Wendy. It is quite impossible to buy a non-padded bra, or find anything larger than a B cup. An entire population of under-endowed women; what a shame!

Once again we were horrified at the snail pace of the Malaysian banks - it must be impossible to serve people any slower. When you enter the door, you get a numbered ticket from a (well hidden) machine. You then sit on a row of stools until your number appears - in my case it was an hour later, and all I wanted to do was change some travellers cheques. Bah! to them.

Georgetown lies at the foot of Penang hill, which rises some 900 metres above sea level to dominate the island. The British originally built a retreat/hospital up there, and over the years several hotels and temples have been added. Nowadays most of the hill has been turned into a nature reserve, and there is a funicular train you can catch to the top, along with assorted touristic type activities at the top to entertain. By far the best reason to make the ascent is because it is significantly cooler at the top. You can catch a bus to the funicular station at the bottom, but don't count on the bus driver to help you get off at the right stop. We were riding along, frantically decoding the traffic signs when we saw one that looked like it said: "Funicular station this way". Since the bus wasn't going that way, we asked the driver if we should get off here to get to the funicular station. His reply: "If you want to". Democratic perhaps, but not terribly useful advice for disorientated tourists!

One afternoon was spent visiting the Cheong Fatt Tze Mansion, built by the famous Chinese businessman of that name in the beginning of the 1900s. The house is built following the principles of Feng Shui; to such an extent that the rear of the house is several brick layers higher than the front, to fulfil the principle of building on a slope. The house is pretty interesting, and the guided tour informative. To quote the pamphlet blurb: "Of his vast empire, Cheong Fatt Tze chose Penang to build the most elaborate of his homes and to raise his sons. It is reputed to be only one of two such buildings of this size, outside of China and certainly the most perfected. While the Mansion's floor plan is essentially Chinese, the overall effect is eclectic and typical of 19th Century Straits Settlements architecture. Gothic louvered windows, Chinese cut-&-paste porcelain work, Stoke-on-Trent floor tiles, Scottish cast iron works and Art Nouveau stained glass are among the features to be found in this inspired work of art. The aura and "chi" of the man pervades the entire edifice". Worth a visit, especially if you are interested in Feng Shui, or the man who forced British steamers to allow Chinese to travel first-class.

We ended up spending almost a week in Penang, enjoying 1st world creature comforts like fast internet connections, a reduced number of hawkers, and a reasonable telecommunications system. Don't miss it if you are in Malaysia; although be warned that using an international calling card in Cantonese requires much guesswork. (That's a tip kids; pray that the English version is working when you are there)

With two days left before our flight to London, we caught the train back to KL, walked for ages in the stinking heat to find that the hotel we were heading for was closed for renovations, had haircuts (!), and ate the most fantastic pulled lamb burger at the Hard Rock Café.

And then, amazingly, after the heavens opened on us on our way to the airport, and eating an aweful McDonalds breakfast, we were on a Malaysian airlines 747 leaving SE Asia behind.

The flight was far too long (15hours), someone added 6 hours to our day, and we got sprayed with disinfectant before we were allowed to land at Heathrow. However, I did win a round of the inflight tivia competition, and the meals were excellent.

Flying over London was amazing - it is *so* big, and has so few tall buildings. Heathrow can only really be described as crap, especially compared to SE Asian airports. However, we were waved into the country with barely a glance at our British passports, and the tube staff were very polite and helpful in getting us the right tickets for our first tube ride. Savouring the accents, and hoping our Rands would stretch far enough, we headed into London…
